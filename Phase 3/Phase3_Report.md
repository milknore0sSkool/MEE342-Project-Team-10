# Introduction and Specifications

A transfer case is used in four-wheel-drive vehicles to distribute input power from the engine or transmission to both the front and rear output shafts. When the case is shifted into 4LOW, both front and rear shafts spin. The output torque increases while the output speed decreases. Meanwhile, when the case is shifted into 4HIGH, the output shafts rotate at the same speed as the input shaft. The final prototype created for this project was intended to withstand an incoming torque of 300 N · m and have a low gear reduction of 2.56:1. The printed assembly is scaled down by half as an educational model that shows the same major mechanical components. 

The main components of the prototype are the housing, input shaft, output shafts, gears, bearings, couplers, and the hand crank assembly. The housing acts as the main support structure and holds the bearings at the correct spacing. The geartrain transfers rotation from the input shaft to the output shafts while maintaining the selected gear ratio. The bearings reduce friction and keep each shaft constrained while allowing rotation.

The final system was modified from the original motor-driven idea to a hand-cranked input for demonstration. A hand crank was connected to the input shaft so that the operator could manually apply torque and rotate the mechanism at a controlled speed. This change simplified the testing procedure, reduced the risk of damaging the 3D printed components, and made the demonstration safer during final testing. The initial modeling, testing, and optimization were all done with the full-scale components in mind and were only scaled for the printing process.

# Design Requirement

The first design requirement was incoming power transmission. The geartrain must withstand an input torque of 300 Nm and transfer motion through the gears without skipping, binding, or excessive backlash. A gear ratio of 2.56:1 was selected so that the output rotation is smooth and complies with automotive design standards. 

The second design requirement was shaft strength and stiffness. Each shaft should have a factor of safety higher than 2. The shaft diameters were checked using Shigley-based design theory in MATLAB.

The third design requirement was bearing support and alignment. The bearings must fit the shaft diameters and sit securely in the 3D printed housing while supporting the weight of each gear and shaft. Poor alignment can be caused by sagging and overstressed gears, which leads to binding, produces uneven wear, and increased friction. 

The fourth requirement was manufacturability through 3D printing. The housing and selected internal components had to be printable while maintaining visual accuracy. This required adding tolerances for bearings, shaft clearance, and coupler meshing. Printed parts had to be oriented properly to improve strength and should not be printed in an orientation that makes them weak along layer lines.

# Shaft Design

The shafts are critical components because they transfer torque while also supporting the gears. Each shaft experiences torsion from the crank and geartrain, bending from gear forces, and reaction forces at the bearings. The final shaft design was checked using Shigley-based shaft design theory in MATLAB. This analysis allowed the team to calculate the internal torque, shear stress, bending moment, normal stress, von Mises stress, and safety factor at critical shaft locations.

The first step in the shaft analysis was to define the free-body diagram for each shaft. The gear force was separated into tangential and radial components. The bearings were modeled as supports, and the reactions at each bearing were calculated from static equilibrium. The tangential gear force contributes to bending in one plane, while the radial gear force contributes to bending in another plane. If the shaft had multiple gears, each force was included at the correct axial location. The resulting bending moments in each plane were combined to find the resultant bending moment at each critical section.

The torsional shear stress was calculated from the shaft torque. For a hollow circular shaft, torsional shear stress increases with torque and decreases strongly with shaft diameter. This is why shaft diameter is one of the most important design variables. A small increase in diameters significantly reduced stress and improved stiffness. The bending normal stress was calculated using the bending moment and the shaft section modulus. MATLAB was used because it can calculate reactions, moment diagrams, and stresses consistently for each shaft.

For our prototype we used 4 shafts for printing purposes and simplifications. When calculating the stresses on each shaft, we decided to constrain all shafts in the middle as a singular shaft with the use of couplers for a total of 3 shafts. However, for the stress analysis, the shafts connected through couplers were modeled as three primary shaft systems: the middle shaft, bottom shaft, and top shaft. This simplified the analysis while still capturing the main torque transfer path and gear loading conditions. The shaft material was assumed to be AISI 4140 steel, which is commonly used for mechanical shafts. Since 4140 steel properties can vary depending on heat treatment, material condition, and data source, the MATLAB analysis used a yield strength of 655 MPa and an ultimate tensile strength of approximately 900 MPa. These values represent a higher-strength 4140 steel condition and were used consistently when calculating the shaft factors of safety. 

Each shaft was modeled with an outer diameter of 30mm and an inner diameter of 12mm. The Input shaft had a 300Nm torque applied to it, this is the force that drove the overall assembly. Due to the complications with ANSYS meshing we decided to initially check if a singular hollow shaft would be able to withstand the initial 300Nm torque in ANSYS. We then continued the analysis using MATLAB to calculate the shear, moment, torque, von misses, and torsion stresses. Following this we calculated the factors of safety in each shaft corresponding to yielding, and fatigue. 

The middle shaft was identified as the critical shaft due to carrying the most gears and experiencing the largest combined effect of torque and bending . The MATLAB results showed that the middle shaft had a maximum Von Mises stress of 283.89 MPa, this result produced a yielding factor of safety of 2.307. This met the team's target result of a safety factor greater than 2. However, the middle shaft also had the lowest fatigue factor of safety at 1.491, making fatigue the limiting concern on this shaft.  \
	The bottom shaft had a maximum von Mises stress of 215.63 MPa, a yielding factor of safety of 3.038, and a fatigue factor of safety of 1.562. The top shaft had a maximum von Mises stress of 258.20 MPa, a yielding factor of safety of 2.537, and a fatigue factor of safety of 2.883. These results show that the selected shaft geometry was acceptable for static loading, but the fatigue margins were not equally strong across all shafts. 

Overall, the shaft analysis showed that yielding was not the main limiting factor because all shafts achieved a factor of safety above 2. Instead, repeated loading and fatigue were more important concerns, especially for the middle shaft. In a future iteration, the middle shaft could be improved by increasing shaft diameter, reducing stress concentrations, improving surface finish, or moving bearing supports closer to the gears to reduce bending moment.

# Bearing Design

The bearings support each shaft and keep the gear mesh aligned while allowing the shafts to rotate with low friction. The bearings also fix the free-spinning gears to the shafts, allowing them to rotate freely about the shaft if they are uncoupled. Bearing design is closely connected to shaft design; the gear forces create bearing reaction loads. If the bearing locations are too far apart, the shaft may deflect more. If the bearings are too close together, the reactions may be higher, and the shaft will not be supported well. The final bearing layout was selected to provide stable shaft support while fitting inside the 3D printed housing. Below is a figure showing the shaft supporting bearing locations.



<p id="gdcalert42" ><span style="color: red; font-weight: bold">>>>>>  gd2md-html alert: inline image link here (to images/image35.png). The bearing loads were calculated from the same free-body diagrams used for the shaft analysis. For each shaft, the radial forces from the gears were applied at the gear locations, and the bearing reactions were solved using static equilibrium. We determined that the best bearing design for a spur gear transfer case is a cylindrical roller bearing, capable of high radial load and extended bearing life. 

Unfortunately, standard cylindrical roller bearings cannot be 3d printed, due to assembly and tolerance. For this reason, we chose to design our very own print-in-place roller bearings. This design choice was made primarily for prototyping purposes and printability. The bearing design features dumbbell shaped rollers, with inner and outer races of a negative profile of the roller. Clearances were kept as low as possible to reduce deflection and “slop” in the bearings, as shown below. Additionally, small 0.25-0.5mm filets and chamfers were added to the edges to reduce print binding, as well as a good press fit onto the shafts and into the bearing housing.

# Phase 2 Comparison

The original 700 Nm design created gear tooth forces that were too large for the selected gear size, shaft spacing, and prototype dimensions. Keeping the transfer case compact while using straight-cut spur gears caused the required gear tooth bending/contact safety factors to become difficult to meet without increasing gear module, face width, shaft diameter, or overall housing size. 

Straight-cut spur gears simplified the design, manufacturing, and analysis because they eliminated axial thrust loads and were easier to model in MATLAB and SolidWorks. However, they also limited how much load the prototype could realistically carry within the selected package size. Helical gears would have provided smoother engagement and improved load sharing across the tooth face, but they would also introduce axial forces, more complex bearing reactions, and more difficult fabrication. 

Although the analytical design was reduced to a 300 Nm input load, the final 3D printed prototype was not physically tested at 300 Nm. Instead, the prototype was used as a scaled functional demonstration to validate gear engagement, shaft rotation, coupler motion, and mode selection between 4WD High, 4WD Low, and 2WD. 


# 3D Printing

The prototype was manufactured using 3D printing. This manufacturing method was appropriate because it allowed the team to create a custom housing, internal supports, and modified geometry without requiring machining. The main design challenge was that printed parts are weaker and less dimensionally accurate than machined metal parts. Therefore, the final design had to account for print orientation, layer adhesion, tolerances, infill, wall thickness, and assembly clearance.

The housing was one of the most important printed components. It had to support the bearings, maintain shaft center distance, and resist cracking during assembly. The housing wall thickness was selected to provide enough stiffness around the bearing pockets and fastener holes. Extra material was kept around high-stress areas such as bearing seats and gear clearance regions. The housing was split into two halves strictly to fit the printer volume. 

The print orientation was also a primary concern to ensure print accuracy and strength. Each shaft was printed vertically to ensure torsional strength and clean inner/ outer surfaces. Each gear was printed on the face to ensure as much accuracy as possible with the gear teeth. The housing was the only component where layering was sacrificed due to its size. The bearing pockets in the housing were not clean curves, yet the z distance and maximum diameter remained consistent. This printing decision saved hundreds of grams in filament supports, while keeping each shaft in plane and concentric with the bearing pockets.

	General printing choices: All prints were completed with a Bambu Lab P1S, 0.4mm nozzle. Black and white prints are basic PLA; the blue components are PETG. Each shaft was printed using a 0.2mm layer height, 20% infill, and a gyroid-shaped infill pattern to ensure bending and torsional strength along the print layers. Each gear was printed using a 0.16 mm layer height to keep the gear teeth accurate and well defined, while not exponentially increasing the print time. The gears used a basic grid infill pattern with 15% infill. This was done primarily to save filament, as the print profile around the gear teeth was thick enough not to be influenced by the infill pattern in this prototype demonstration.

Post-printing discussion: Overall, the prints turned out very well. The gear teeth were printed clean and strong. The face widths were dimensionally accurate to +/-0.05 mm. Shaft diameters were each within -0.1 of the specified diameter to ensure the bearing could fit. The housing is the least aesthetically pleasing component due to the design choices mentioned above, but it functioned extremely well for the intended purpose. The bearings were our biggest issue. Due to the bearing design, there was a considerable amount of deflection and a small amount of allowable translation in each direction. The rollers in the bearings were designed with 0.15 mm clearance from the inner and outer races, which was necessary to ensure the rollers do not bind together or to the races when printing. Post printing, some edges of the bearings were linked together; they had to be lightly pulled apart. This was expected, but fixable; the small adhesion between the rollers left imperfections, which gradually got smoothed out the more they were used.

# Assembly

All parts in the assembly were 3d printed, and each component was fixed to its respective place using superglue and calipers. The first part to assemble is the case, which was printed in two parts to fit within the 256 mm^3 print volume. Each half was lined up and glued together. Next, each bearing and gear was fixed to the shafts with super glue at the specified locations per our design, measured to ensure consistency. The next step of the assembly is to glue the support bearings on the ends of each shaft to the support locations on the shaft. Finally, attach the crank handle to the input shaft.  \

# Testing

After printing the final components, the assembled transfer case was tested using a hand crank attached to the input shaft. The prototype was tested in each operating configuration, including 4WD High, 4WD Low, and 2WD. For each stage, the input shaft was rotated manually and the output shaft rotations were observed to determine whether the motion matched the expected gear ratio. Overall, the prototype was able to transfer rotation through the selected gear stages, and the observed output motion was consistent with the intended gear ratio for each configuration.

However, several assembly and tolerance issues affected the smoothness of operation. Because the 3D printed couplers were produced without sufficient clearance, they did not always engage cleanly with the intended gear stage. Similar tolerance issues were observed at the bearing locations. The bearings had excessive axial movement, which allowed the shafts and gears to shift during operation. This movement prevented the gears from maintaining consistent mesh contact at all times. As the hand crank speed increased, the axial movement became more noticeable, causing less consistent gear engagement and occasional rough rotation. These results showed that the main limitation of the prototype was not the basic gear ratio design, but the manufacturing tolerances of the rotating components. 


# Bill of Materials and Final Optimization

The bill of materials summarizes the final components used in the prototype. This includes printed parts, purchased hardware, shafts, bearings, fasteners, gears, and the hand crank. The BOM is important because it shows that the design is not only theoretical but also buildable. A bill of materials also aids in finalizing manufacturing and maintenance cost and helps identify each component of the assembly when examining failure points and maintenance. 

The final optimization process focused on making the prototype function reliably under hand-cranked loading. The most important optimization was changing the input from a motor to a hand crank. This reduced the input speed, lowered the risk of sudden failure, and gave the operator direct feedback if the mechanism started to bind. This change also simplified the electronics because no motor controller, power supply, or motor mount was required. The hand crank made the demonstration more appropriate for a printed prototype because the loading could be applied gradually.

A second optimization was improving component alignment. Transfer cases are sensitive to shaft spacing because the gears must mesh properly. In the final assembly we ensured that all shafts and components moved without restriction and aligned properly with each step of assembly. When assembling the final prototype, the designed tolerances and overall optimization allowed for the assembly to function as intended upon printing. A lubricant as well as an adhesive was used to join the parts together. 

A third optimization was balancing strength and print time. Thicker walls and higher infill improve stiffness but increase material use and print time. Lower infill saves time but may allow bearing pockets or screw bosses to deform. The final print settings should represent a compromise between strength, cost, and manufacturability. Due to a scaling issue with the input shaft and one of the gears when printing, a reprint was necessary for the operation of the prototype. 

Overall, the final design met the objective of creating a functional transfer case demonstration. The prototype shows how torque enters through an input shaft, transfers through the geartrain, and exits through supported output shafts. The Shigley-based MATLAB analysis provided engineering justification for the shaft and gear dimensions. However, final optimization could have been slightly more accurate however we did come with many issues typically stemming from ANSYS Student Version. Due to complications with ANSYS Student Version, we weren’t able to accurately mesh the gear contact areas with enough precision which would lead to extremely deviated results. Therefore Shigley’s Gear Design was used as a reference to fully optimize dimensions, stress, and be able to create a desirable factor of safety within the assembly. While the 3D printed prototype demonstrated the practical manufacturing and assembly challenges of turning the design into a physical mechanism. Future improvements could include a stronger printed material, metal shafts with machined flats or keyways, improved bearing retention, a transparent housing cover, lubrication, and a controlled motor input once the printed parts are proven reliable. Traditional transfer cases also contain many additional components such as clutches which would’ve allowed us to more accurately demonstrate an actual transfer case. Due to the complexity of all the components normally integrated within a transfer case and time constraints, we had to carefully choose what we were going to prioritize as a team.

Given more time and resources, a deeper analysis could be done at a certain RPM range, and how the transfer case would react when external forces are applied, such as towing. Power loss due to friction and oil lubrication is something that was not accounted for along with the weight of the gears. Deeper analysis would yield results slightly lower if those new parameters were implemented.
